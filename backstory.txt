1. Sree Padmanabhaswamy Temple

The temple's origins are traced to the Treta Yuga, when the sage Vilvamangalam (Divakara Muni) encountered a mischievous child on the banks of the Ananthankadu forest who was actually Lord Vishnu himself. The child disappeared into a large Iluppa tree (Indian Butter Tree), which then fell and transformed into the massive reclining Vishnu idol — so large it spans the length of three cells (the head, torso and feet each visible through separate windows called thrikkaals). The Travancore royal family adopted the title Padmanabhadasa (servant of Padmanabha), dedicating the entire kingdom to the deity in 1750 CE under Maharaja Anizham Thirunal Marthanda Varma. The vaults (Kallara A through F) beneath the temple gained world attention in 2011 when the Supreme Court-ordered inventory revealed treasures estimated at over ₹1 lakh crore — gold idols, diamond necklaces, Napoleonic coins and ancient manuscripts — making it the wealthiest place of worship ever documented. Vault B remains unopened, believed by the royal family to be sealed by a Naga Bandham (serpent charm) that only a specific Garuda Mantra can safely dissolve.


2. Guruvayur Sree Krishna Temple

According to the Narayaneeyam composed by Melpathur Narayana Bhattathiri, the idol of Guruvayurappan is the same four-armed Vishnu idol originally worshipped by Brahma, then by Kashyapa and Devaki (Krishna's parents), and finally by Krishna himself in Dwarka. When Dwarka was submerging into the sea after Krishna's departure from the world, Uddhava — Krishna's closest disciple — was instructed by Vishnu to have Brihaspati (Guru, preceptor of the gods) and Vayu (the wind god) carry the idol to a sacred spot chosen by Lord Shiva. Shiva and Parvati, who were residing at the Rudratirtha lake in Kerala, rose and welcomed them, and the spot where the idol was consecrated became Guru-Vayu-r — the town of Guru and Vayu. The great saint Vilwamangalam Swamiyar is credited with actually seeing the child Krishna playing in the temple in the 14th century, and his encounter legends are woven into the temple's oral tradition. Poonthanam's Jnanappana and Melpathur's Narayaneeyam — both composed while their authors were at Guruvayur seeking cures — are among the greatest devotional works in Malayalam and Sanskrit respectively.


3. Sabarimala Sree Dharmasastha Temple

The legend of Sabarimala centers on Manikandan, a prince of the Pandalam royal dynasty who was actually Dharmasastha — the divine son born of the union of Shiva and Mohini (Vishnu's female form). Abandoned as an infant on the banks of the Pampa river, he was found and raised by the childless king of Pandalam. As he grew, his divine nature became evident through miraculous acts. When the king wished to crown him, Manikandan revealed his true identity and walked into the forest to the hill of Sabarimala, where he merged back into the divine. Before departing, he granted his foster father the right to conduct his worship. He also defeated the demoness Mahishi — who could only be killed by someone born of both Shiva and Vishnu — on the hill of Nilackal. The eighteen sacred steps (Pathinettampadi) leading to the sanctum represent the eighteen hills surrounding Sabarimala and the eighteen puranas. The 41-day Vratha (penance) pilgrims observe — wearing black, abstaining from meat and pleasures — mirrors Manikandan's own austerities. The Makaravilakku, a star that appears in the sky on Makaravilakku night, is believed to be a divine celestial signal accompanying the deity's festival.


4. Attukal Bhagavathy Temple

The presiding deity is identified as Kannaki — the heroine of the Tamil Sangam epic Silappatikaram — on her journey from Madurai northward after destroying the city with her divine fury following the unjust execution of her husband Kovalan. According to local tradition, Kannaki rested at Attukal on her way and revealed herself to a local devotee named Karthyayani in a dream, asking to be enshrined there. The deity is thus a rare fusion — the fierce Tamil goddess of chastity and justice merged with the Kerala tradition of Bhagavathi worship. The Attukal Pongala, held on the ninth day of the Malayalam month of Karkidakam, is the ritual expression of this story — millions of women cook pongala (rice pudding) on open hearths on the streets of Thiruvananthapuram as an offering, recreating the communal cooking associated with Kannaki's story. In 2009 it entered the Guinness World Records with over 2.5 million women participating in a single religious gathering — the largest such assembly of women on earth.


5. Vadakkunnathan Temple

Vadakkunnathan — Lord of the North — is one of the Parasurama Kshetras, temples said to have been consecrated by Parasurama, the sixth avatar of Vishnu who, having reclaimed Kerala from the sea by throwing his axe into the ocean, built 108 Shiva temples across the land. Vadakkunnathan is considered the foremost of these. The temple is built in the classic Kerala Thachu Shastra style with no metal nails — entirely wood, laterite and copper — and its murals depicting scenes from the Mahabharata are considered masterpieces of the Kerala school. The deity here is unusual: Lord Shiva is worshipped not as a Shivalinga but as a form concealed beneath a massive mound of ghee — accumulated over centuries of abhishekam (ritual anointing) — so that the original idol is no longer visible, only a golden ghee mountain. The Thrissur Pooram, held in the Thekkinkadu Maidan surrounding the temple, is not a temple festival in the traditional sense but a competitive festival between ten surrounding temples — each bringing their own caparisoned elephants, percussion orchestras and parasol displays — instituted by Sakthan Thampuran of Kochi in the late 18th century after unifying all local temple festivals into one grand spectacle.


6. Ambalapuzha Sree Krishna Temple

The most beloved legend of Ambalapuzha concerns a game of chess. The presiding deity Lord Krishna, disguised as a wandering sage, challenged the local king to a game. The king, confident of winning, agreed to whatever prize the sage named. The sage asked for rice — one grain on the first square of the chessboard, two on the second, four on the third, doubling each time for all 64 squares. The king laughed at the seemingly modest request but soon realized it was mathematically impossible to fulfill — the total grains required would exceed all the rice in the world several times over. Krishna then revealed himself and said the debt would be honored daily — and so palpayasam (sweet rice pudding cooked in milk and sugar) has been offered to the deity at Ambalapuzha every single day since, as partial repayment of that infinite debt. The payasam of Ambalapuzha is famous across Kerala and devotees believe no payasam made elsewhere can match its taste. The temple itself belongs to the Divya Desam tradition and is known for the Pana festival and the unique Kettukazhcha — enormous decorated floats carried in procession.


7. Aranmula Parthasarathy Temple

Aranmula is one of the five Krishna temples (Pandavarkavu) in central Kerala said to have been consecrated by the Pandavas after the Kurukshetra war as penance for the lives lost. Arjuna himself is said to have installed the deity here — Parthasarathy, meaning charioteer of Arjuna, one of Krishna's most intimate epithets. The temple sits on the banks of the Pampa river and gives its name to the Aranmula Kannadi — the metal-alloy mirror unique to this village, whose formula is a closely guarded secret passed down within a single family for generations. The Uthrittathi Vallamsankedam — the snake boat race held here — is unlike the competitive Nehru Trophy race; it is a votive offering. Boats from surrounding villages carry offerings to the temple in a procession on the Pampa, and it is considered a ritual act of devotion rather than a sporting contest. The race is always held on the Uthrittathi asterism in the Malayalam month of Chingam (August–September) and is believed to have been instituted by a local chieftain whose boat procession was disrupted by floods, requiring all villages to bring offerings together.


8. Ettumanoor Mahadeva Temple

The name Ettumanoor means the place of eight men in Malayalam — a reference to the legend of eight hunters who, while pursuing game, witnessed the self-manifested (swayambhu) Shivalinga glowing in the forest and fell into divine trance before the deity. Shiva appeared and consecrated himself at that spot. The temple's Ezharaponnana — seven and a half golden elephants — are among the most celebrated temple treasures in Kerala. The half-elephant is particularly significant: legend holds that a poor devotee who could only afford to offer half an elephant's worth of gold in fulfillment of a vow was accepted with equal grace by Shiva, establishing the teaching that devotion matters more than wealth. The wall murals inside the temple, painted in the Kerala style using natural pigments, include an extraordinarily detailed depiction of Tripuradahana (Shiva destroying the three cities) and scenes from the Mahabharata considered among the finest surviving examples of traditional Kerala mural art — comparable in quality to the murals of Mattancherry Palace. The Ettumanoor Ezhunoollal, the ten-day festival, draws enormous crowds to witness the spectacle of caparisoned elephants.


9. Mannarasala Sree Nagaraja Temple

Mannarasala's origin story begins with the sage Kashyapa and his wife Kadru, who were the progenitors of all serpents. One branch of their lineage settled in the dense forest along the backwaters of Haripad, where the serpent deity Nagaraja made his home in an ancient grove. A Brahmin family settled nearby, and the lady of the house — Valiamma — began worshipping the serpents in the grove with extraordinary devotion. Nagaraja appeared to her and promised that as long as the eldest woman of her lineage remained celibate and devoted, the grove would remain sacred and childless couples who prayed here would receive the blessing of children. This is why even today the chief priest of Mannarasala is a woman — the Amma of the family — making it one of the very few temples in Kerala with a female head priest, a tradition maintained unbroken for generations. The 16-acre grove contains over 30,000 stone serpent idols (nagakkallu) placed by devotees over centuries. The Ayilya festival draws thousands of childless couples who offer serpent idols and pray for progeny. No tree in the grove may be cut and no animal may be harmed within its boundaries.


10. Thirunavaya Navamukunda Temple

Thirunavaya on the banks of the Bharatapuzha (Nila river) was the site of the Mamankam — one of the greatest festivals in medieval Kerala history, held once every twelve years and comparable in scale and significance to the Kumbh Mela. For over 800 years, the Mamankam was a political and religious event where the Zamorin of Kozhikode presided as sovereign. Rival chieftains, particularly the Valluvanad rulers, would send warriors called Chaaverukal (death-squads) — men who had taken a vow to die attempting to kill the Zamorin's representative — in an act of political defiance disguised as ritual bravado. None ever succeeded, but their suicidal charge became legendary. The festival ceased after the last recorded Mamankam in 1755 CE, partly due to the destabilization caused by the invasion of Hyder Ali. The presiding deity is Navamukunda — Vishnu in his nine-formed aspect — and the temple is one of the 108 Divya Desams. The Bharatapuzha at this point is considered supremely sacred for pitrutarpana (ancestral rites), and the Thirunavaya Ghat is among the most important sites in Kerala for performing death rituals.


11. Kotarakkara Mahaganapathy Temple

Kotarakkara's Ganapathy temple is inseparable from the legend of Perunthachan — the master architect of Kerala, whose name literally means the Great Carpenter and who is considered the father of Kerala's traditional Thachu Shastra (science of woodcraft and temple architecture). According to legend, Perunthachan built temples and palaces across Kerala with a skill no one could match, and his son — also a gifted craftsman — began to rival and eventually surpass him. Unable to bear being outdone by his own child, Perunthachan devised a cruel test: while his son was working atop a tall temple thazhikam (finial), Perunthachan deliberately made a small mistake in a measurement below, knowing his son would instinctively correct it by shifting his weight — causing him to fall to his death. Overcome by grief and guilt, Perunthachan came to Kotarakkara and performed penance before Mahaganapathy, seeking absolution. The deity is therefore seen as the remover of guilt and the god of craftsmen, and Kotarakkara became a place of pilgrimage for Kerala's traditional craftsmen — carpenters, sculptors and architects — who pray here before beginning major works.


12. Thiruvarppu Krishna Temple

Thiruvarppu, on the banks of the Meenachil river in Kottayam, is believed to be the site where the Pandavas spent a period of their exile. The presiding deity Krishna is worshipped here as Karunakaramoorthy — the embodiment of compassion — and the idol is said to have been installed by Bhima, the second Pandava. The river bend at Thiruvarppu (the name possibly derived from Thiru-Varp meaning sacred bend) was a place where the Pandavas performed daily worship. A local legend describes Bhima fashioning the idol from a single piece of wood from the riverside forest and consecrating it himself. The temple follows the Bharatapuzha tradition of Krishna worship and is particularly associated with the Vishu festival when the deity is believed to bless devotees with abundance for the coming year. The temple's antiquity is attested by its mention in medieval Kerala chronicles and by the distinctive style of its consecrated image.


13. Beemapally Mosque (syncretic significance)

Beemapally near Thiruvananthapuram is the dargah of Beema Beevi — a Muslim woman saint believed to have possessed miraculous healing powers and who died in the 17th century. The Chandanakudam festival held here annually is one of the most remarkable expressions of Kerala's syncretic culture: Hindu devotees participate in equal numbers alongside Muslim worshippers, bringing offerings of sandalwood paste (chandanam) and coconuts. The festival culminates in a procession with caparisoned elephants — a practice borrowed directly from Kerala Hindu temple tradition — making it one of the very few Islamic shrines in the world where elephants form part of the religious procession. The shared reverence reflects the centuries of peaceful coexistence along Kerala's Malabar and Travancore coasts, where port trade created communities where religious boundaries were often fluid.


14. Chettikulangara Bhagavathy Temple

The legend of Chettikulangara begins with a merchant (Chetti in Malayalam) from a trading community who found a divine light emanating from a termite mound (kula) near a pond (kulangara meaning pond-side). When excavated, the mound revealed a self-manifested idol of Bhagavathi. The merchant enshrined her and the goddess promised to protect the region. The Karthika Vilakku — the lamp festival of Karthika — is the temple's most spectacular event, when tens of thousands of oil lamps are lit simultaneously across the temple premises and the surrounding streets, creating a sea of fire visible for miles. The festival is also known for the Kettukazhcha — enormous effigies (ketti) made of bamboo, cloth and paper, built by competing neighborhoods in the form of mythological scenes, chariots and animals, and carried in a grand procession to be offered to the goddess.


15. Kodungallur Bhagavathy Temple (Sree Kurumba)

Kodungallur — ancient Muchiri or Cranganore — was Kerala's greatest port city in the early centuries CE, the entry point of Jews, Christians, Muslims and spices traders to the subcontinent. The Bhagavathy here is identified with Kurumba — a fierce form of the goddess associated with the ancient Dravidian tradition — and also with Kannaki (of Silappatikaram) who is said to have finally been deified here after her journey northward from Madurai. The Bharani festival is the most controversial and celebrated of Kerala's temple festivals: devotees called Kuruthiyattakkar arrive singing deliberately crude, irreverent songs (Bharani pattu) — a ritualized inversion of normal decorum that is understood as an offering of raw humanity to the fierce goddess. In ancient times the offering to the goddess involved blood; today it is symbolic. The festival represents the meeting point of Brahminic and tribal traditions — the goddess here has always been more wild than domesticated, accepting what more refined traditions would refuse.


16. Thrikkakkara Vamana Temple

Thrikkakkara is the only major Vamana (dwarf avatar of Vishnu) temple in Kerala and sits at the very heart of the Onam origin story. According to the Puranas, the benevolent asura king Mahabali ruled Kerala in a golden age of perfect equality and abundance — so perfect that the gods grew jealous of his popularity and complained to Vishnu. Vishnu incarnated as Vamana, a small Brahmin boy, and approached Mahabali during a great yajna (sacrifice). Mahabali's preceptor Shukracharya warned him not to grant the boy's boon, but Mahabali — true to his fame for generosity — promised to give whatever Vamana asked. Vamana asked for three steps of land. Mahabali agreed. Vamana then grew to cosmic proportions: with one step he covered the earth, with the second the heavens, and for the third he placed it on Mahabali's head and pushed him into the netherworld (Patala). Moved by Mahabali's nobility, Vishnu granted him the boon to visit his beloved subjects once a year — that annual return is Onam. Thrikkakkara is believed to be where Vamana's cosmic foot first touched the earth, and the Thrikkakkarappan idol carried in Onam celebrations across Kerala is a small clay figure representing the deity of this temple.


17. Lokanarkavu Temple

Lokanarkavu Bhagavathy temple at Vatakara in Malabar is one of the Nalambalam (four sacred temples) of northern Kerala. The presiding goddess Lokanarkavu Amma is considered the clan deity of the Kottayil Kovilakom — a branch of the Zamorin's royal family — and is deeply embedded in the martial history of the Malabar coast. The temple region was historically the training ground of the Kalari tradition — the ancient martial art of Kerala — and the goddess was invoked by warriors before battle. The Zamorin's celebrated Nair warriors of the Malabar coast traced their protection to this goddess. The temple is known for the ancient Kaliyattam and Theyyam traditions that predate Brahminic influence in this region — making it a living connection to Kerala's pre-Aryan religious traditions where the goddess was not a consort but a sovereign power in her own right.


18. Mammiyoor Mahadeva Temple

Mammiyoor Shiva temple in Guruvayur town is inseparable from Guruvayur Krishna temple — the two are considered a single sacred unit, and Shiva here is said to be the guardian of Guruvayur. The legend says that when Brihaspati and Vayu brought the Krishna idol and consecrated it at Guruvayur (at Shiva's invitation), Shiva himself took up residence at Mammiyoor — just 500 meters away — to serve as protector of the Krishna shrine. Devotees traditionally visit Mammiyoor first to seek Shiva's blessings before entering Guruvayur's sanctum, making Mammiyoor effectively the dwara palaka (doorkeeper) of one of India's most visited temples. The Shivalinga at Mammiyoor is considered swayambhu (self-manifested) and predates the Guruvayur Krishna temple. The Mammiyoor Ekadasi — held the day after Guruvayur Ekadasi — draws enormous crowds, and circumambulating both temples together (pradakshina) is considered a complete act of worship.


19. Thirumandhamkunnu Bhagavathy Temple

Thirumandhamkunnu in Malappuram is one of the most powerful goddess shrines of Malabar, perched on a forested hill. The goddess here is Ambika — a form of Parvati — and the temple has a distinctly northern Kerala character, with strong connections to the Nair and Ezhava communities of Malabar. The Bharani festival here — similar in structure to Kodungallur's Bharani — involves a massive gathering where devotees climb the hill in a procession, and was historically associated with the martial communities of the region who sought the goddess's blessing before campaigns. The temple's hill location (kunnu = hill, mandham = sacred grove) reflects the ancient practice of goddess worship on high ground, considered the goddess's natural domain in the pre-temple traditions of Kerala. Local belief holds that the goddess here and the goddess at Kodungallur are sisters, and that the two Bharani festivals — though geographically distant — are spiritually connected.


20. Kottiyoor Shiva Temple

Kottiyoor in Kannur district is one of Kerala's most ancient and unusual Shiva temples — it exists in two forms: Akkare Kottiyoor (on the far bank) which is the original and more sacred shrine, accessible only during the annual Vysakha Mahotsavam festival for 28 days, and Ikkare Kottiyoor (on the near bank) which is open year-round. The original shrine has no roof, no walls, and no permanent idol — the deity is worshipped as a self-manifested Shivalinga in an open forest clearing beside the Bavali river, exposed to rain, sun and sky. This is deliberate: the legend connects Kottiyoor to the Daksha Yaga — the great sacrifice organized by Daksha (Parvati's father) to which Shiva was not invited. When Parvati arrived uninvited and was humiliated by her father's contempt for Shiva, she immolated herself. In his grief and rage, Shiva destroyed the sacrifice. It is said that pieces of Daksha's destroyed yagashala (sacrifice hall) fell across Kerala, and Kottiyoor is where the sacred fire itself landed — which is why the shrine remains open to the elements, as the original yagna was an open-air event. The river water is considered as sacred as the Ganga here during the festival, and hundreds of thousands of pilgrims trek through forest to reach the site.


21. Kumaranalloor Devi Temple

Aithihyamala tells the famous story behind the phrase that the goddess "came running" to Kumaranalloor. The deity was originally worshipped as Madurai Meenakshi under the Pandya kings. When a precious nose ornament disappeared from the idol, the innocent priest was accused and ordered to recover it within forty days or face death. On the final night, a divine voice urged him to flee. As he ran through the darkness, a radiant woman followed and then moved ahead of him, lighting the way with her own brilliance. The priest eventually slept in a wayside shelter and woke inside the newly built Kumaranalloor temple, where the same divine woman was seated in the sanctum. Cheraman Perumal, who had planned to install Subrahmanya there and Bhagavathi at Udayanapuram, first resisted this unexpected presence. But when a strange mist blocked his journey and cleared only after he promised land and honours to the goddess, he accepted her will. A dream later revealed another idol in a nearby well, and that image was installed at the auspicious hour. Thus Kumaranalloor became the seat of the goddess who left Madurai with her devotee and claimed the shrine for herself.


22. Thirunakkara Mahadeva Temple

The Aithihyamala legend links Thirunakkara Mahadeva to the devotion of a Thekkumkur ruler who regularly worshipped Vadakkunnathan at Thrissur. When age and illness made the journey impossible, the king prayed in sorrow before Vadakkunnathan. That night he dreamt that Shiva would come to Thirunakkara hill, marked by a bull in front and a white ixora shrub behind. On the return journey the king also met a poor Vaikom Namboothiri whose devotion moved him deeply. The story then ties together the king's piety, the divine promise, and the discovery of Shiva's presence at Thirunakkara. The temple's beloved sacred bull is remembered in the same tradition, making Thirunakkara not merely a royal foundation but a shrine where Shiva is believed to have come closer to an ageing devotee who could no longer travel to Thrissur.


23. Pazhoor Perumthrikkovil Temple

Aithihyamala gives Pazhoor Perumthrikkovil an origin rooted in astrology, fear, and an act of merit. A Namboothiri once took horoscopes to the famed Pazhoor astrologer, who saw a grave danger to his life within a year. Distressed, the Namboothiri resolved in his heart to build a Shiva temple, endow its daily worship, and dedicate the needed wealth for the deity. When he returned to the astrologer the next morning, the horoscope had changed: the danger had passed and long life was now visible, because a great punya karma had already been formed by his sincere resolve. The Namboothiri then fulfilled that vow. A skilled carpenter designed the shrine on the model of Vaikom Mahadeva Temple and chose a site on the bank of the east-flowing Pazhoor river. Shiva was consecrated there with daily pujas, sheeveli, navakam, panchagavyam, and the regular observances of a major temple. This is the legend of how Pazhoor Perumthrikkovil came into being.


24. Pakkil Sree Dharma Sastha Temple

Aithihyamala traces Pakkil Sastha to a remarkable meeting of Brahma, Agni, Parashurama, and Pakkanar. Brahma is said to have given a Sastha idol from his golden sacrificial vessel to Agni, asking him to deliver it to Parashurama for installation in a worthy place. Parashurama reached the place later called Pakkil and tried to consecrate the idol, but the image would not stay fixed. At that moment Pakkanar and his wife arrived. Hearing Parashurama's difficulty, Pakkanar pressed the idol down and commanded it to "stay here" — "ivide parkku." The idol became firm, and the place was remembered as Parkku, later Pakkil. Because of this layered legend, people variously say the deity was installed by Parashurama, by Pakkanar, or through Agni's agency. After the consecration Sastha is believed to have appeared to Pakkanar and asked that he visit at least once a year, which became part of the temple's living tradition.


25. Aranmula Parthasarathy Temple

Aithihyamala presents Aranmula as an ancient and powerful Parthasarathy shrine on the Pampa, with the deity believed to have been installed by Arjuna. The account especially emphasizes Krishna's affection for children and childlike offerings. Feeding children in the name of Aranmula Devan, known as Aranmula Oottu, is described as a pleasing vow for the deity and is performed for the welfare of children, recovery from illness, and fulfilment of prayers. The text also explains local customs connected with children collecting areca palm sheaths, lighting the ceremonial fire before Makara Sankranti, and the sacred fish at the temple ghat, called the deity's own children. These fish are not harmed; devotees feed them coconut and rice as a vow, especially for blessings connected with childbirth and nursing. The story expands Aranmula's backstory beyond architecture and boat processions into a temple culture where Krishna's grace is expressed through children, food, river, fish, and community obligation.


26. Sasthamkotta Sree Sasta Temple

Aithihyamala explains Sasthamkotta through a Pandalam king devoted to Sabarimala Sastha. The king had married a Kayamkulam princess and stayed away from Pandalam and Sabarimala for twelve years. Troubling dreams and cries of tigers and leopards were interpreted as the displeasure of his family deity. After he returned to Sabarimala for worship, Sastha appeared in a dream and promised that the king need not leave his beloved queen every month to come to Sabarimala; the deity would come closer and reside nearby. The place that had been called Kotta became Sasthamkotta because of Sastha's presence. The same legend explains the temple's famous monkeys. They served as divine attendants of the king and queen, received regular food from the temple, and even recovered a royal ring carried away by a bird. The monkeys are therefore treated not as ordinary animals but as members of Sastha's retinue.


27. Mannarasala Sree Nagaraja Temple

Aithihyamala gives Mannarasala a cosmic Kerala origin. When Parashurama brought Brahmins to Kerala, the land was troubled by serpents and the water was salty. Shiva advised him to propitiate Vasuki, king of serpents. Vasuki agreed to withdraw the salt from the waters and gather the serpents into sacred groves, provided Kerala households honoured serpent deities and preserved their kavus. Parashurama then consecrated Nagaraja, Nagayakshi, and many serpent powers in the forest where he had performed austerities, setting aside the grove and entrusting its worship to a resident Brahmin family. Later, when the Khandava forest fire spread toward the serpent grove, the women of that family saved the serpents by pouring water until the heated earth cooled. A divine voice declared that the soil had cooled — "mannu aari" — and the place became Mannarishala, later Mannarasala. The tradition that the senior woman of the family conducts serpent worship is tied to this gratitude of the serpent powers toward the women who protected them.


28. Chengannur Mahadeva Temple

Aithihyamala says the main presence at Chengannur is Shiva with Parvati, yet the fame of the place belongs especially to Bhagavathi, as in many Kerala shrines where the goddess's power is most visibly experienced. The origin is linked to Parvati's wedding. When Shiva, Brahma, Vishnu, the gods, sages, celestial beings, and hosts of devotees gathered in the Himalayas for the divine marriage, the earth tilted under the weight in the north. The gods asked Agastya Maharshi to go south and remain on the southern bank of the Pampa at Shonadri until the wedding assembly dispersed. Chengannur's sacred geography is connected to that southern balancing act. Aithihyamala also preserves the striking tradition of the goddess's periodic menstruation and the Thiruppootharattu observance. When a later official dismissed the custom and removed the expenses for the ritual from the temple accounts, his wife is said to have suffered bleeding that would not stop until the goddess was honoured. The story explains why Chengannur Bhagavathi's embodied feminine power is central to the temple's identity.


29. Achankovil Sree Dharmasastha Temple

Aithihyamala describes Achankovil as one of the ancient and important Sastha shrines of the old Travancore region, located in the valley below the Achankovil hills. The presiding deity is Sastha, honoured by the Pandalam royal family as their kuladevata. The account is notable for its detailed description of the deity's retinue: Bhagavathi inside the temple precinct, two additional goddess forms outside, a Vishnu image near the eastern side, Karuppaswamy and Karuppayi Amma facing west, Cheppani Madan, Kala Madan, Kochittanan, and Singili Bhoothathan. These attendant powers are treated as Sastha's protective circle, with Karuppaswamy especially important. The legend gives Achankovil its character as a forest-edge royal Sastha shrine where the main deity is inseparable from a powerful family of guardian deities.


30. Kodungallur Bhagavathy Temple

Aithihyamala's Kodungallur chapter focuses on Vasoorimala, a fierce smallpox-related goddess associated with the Bhagavathi tradition. It begins with the Puranic battle in which Bhadrakali, born from Shiva's third eye, destroys the asura Daruka. Daruka's wife Manodari performs severe tapas near Kailasa and obtains from Shiva the terrible power connected with pox. In Kerala folk memory this power becomes Vasoorimala, invoked and feared in relation to epidemics, oracles, and the fierce devotional world of Kodungallur. The story helps explain why Kodungallur Bhagavathi is not a gentle domestic goddess but a blazing protective force, close to disease, possession, battle, and raw speech. The temple's Bharani atmosphere, velichappadu traditions, and fierce goddess worship all belong to this older imagination of Bhadrakali's power.


31. Panachikkadu Saraswathi Temple

Aithihyamala remembers Panachikkadu as a Saraswathi shrine whose grace drew royal attention and lasting ritual endowments. The story says that when the ruler came for darshan, he offered an ornament of five strands to Saraswathi and a gold-set coral garland to Vishnu. He also arranged daily offerings: milk payasam with sugar for Saraswathi, and separate cooked offerings and payasam for Vishnu, with portions distributed to Brahmins and temple servants according to custom. The legend preserves the temple's dual sacred character: Saraswathi as the visible power of learning and speech, and Vishnu as the companion presence within the shrine. This is why Panachikkadu is remembered not merely as a village temple but as Kerala's Dakshina Mookambika, a place where learning is ritually fed every day.


32. Ammathiruvadi Temple

Aithihyamala connects Urakam Ammathiruvadi with the devotion of a Namboothiri belonging to the Perumanam Brahmin settlement. He used to worship Thirumandhamkunnu Bhagavathi with deep faith and prayed that the same divine presence might come nearer to his home. The goddess is said to have consented and accompanied him back in a palm-leaf umbrella. On the way, he rested at Urakam; when he tried to lift the umbrella again, it would not move. Understanding this as the goddess's will, he invoked her there and established worship. The tradition therefore remembers Urakam Ammathiruvadi as a shrine where Bhagavathi chose to remain out of grace for a devotee, carrying the living connection of Thirumandhamkunnu into central Kerala.


33. Sree Poornathrayeesa Temple

Aithihyamala's Tripunithura account treats Sree Poornathrayeesa as the royal Vishnu shrine at the heart of Cochin's old sacred capital. The temple's festivals are remembered not merely as public celebrations but as acts of royal and civic devotion, where the ruler, temple servants, musicians, ritual specialists, and the people of Tripunithura all had defined roles. The deity, Poornathrayeesa, is understood as Vishnu in a complete and auspicious form, and the festival traditions show the bond between the deity and the old Cochin kingdom. This backstory is therefore best read as a temple-tradition note rather than a single miraculous origin story, because the Aithihyamala account mainly preserves festival memory, royal custom, and the shrine's honoured place in Tripunithura.


34. Vaikom Mahadeva Temple

Aithihyamala contains Vaikom-related devotional traditions rather than a simple origin story. Vaikom Mahadeva is remembered as one of Kerala's most revered Shiva shrines, with temple life shaped by vows, offerings, learned families, and stories of devotees whose humility was accepted by the deity. In these accounts, the power of Vaikom is not only in the sanctum but in the way Mahadeva responds to sincere worship, disciplined service, and acts of faith. This makes Vaikom's backstory a respectful tradition note: a shrine where Shiva is approached through devotion, restraint, service, and the inherited rituals of Kerala temple culture.


35. Aluva Mahadeva Temple

[Festival / Ritual]

Aluva Mahadeva Temple is especially known for Aluva Sivarathri on the Manappuram sand bank of the Periyar. Devotees keep vigil through the night with Shiva worship, chanting, hymns, scriptural readings, and special pujas. On the morning after Sivarathri, many pilgrims offer bali tharpanam for departed ancestors on the river bank, making the festival both a Shiva observance and a deeply personal act of remembrance. The Manappuram also becomes a large festival ground with temporary stalls, cultural programmes, processions, pallivetta, aarattu, and a fair that traditionally continues beyond the main Sivarathri day.


36. Kottankulangara Sree Bhagavathy Temple

[Festival / Ritual]

Kottankulangara Sree Bhagavathy Temple at Chavara is famous for Chamayavilakku, held in the Malayalam month of Meenam. The central vow is striking and devotional: male devotees dress in traditional female attire, carry lamps, and join the night procession to seek the blessings of Bhagavathy, also revered here as Vanadurga. The ritual is treated with respect as an offering of humility, beauty, and surrender before the goddess. The festival also includes traditional music, illuminations, processions, the kuruthola panthal, cultural programmes, and fireworks, and it has become an important inclusive space for many devotees, including members of Kerala's transgender community.


37. Sree Visalakshi Sametha Viswanathaswamy Temple

[Festival / Ritual]

Kalpathy Ratholsavam is the great chariot festival of Sree Visalakshi Sametha Viswanathaswamy Temple at Kalpathy in Palakkad. The temple stands in the old Tamil Brahmin agraharam, and the festival transforms the village streets into a sacred route for decorated temple chariots. After the flag-hoisting, the deities are taken in grand rathams pulled by devotees through the agraharam, accompanied by music, prayer, and community participation. The observance is also known for its Carnatic music programmes and for preserving the living link between temple worship, agraharam culture, and Palakkad's shared Kerala-Tamil heritage.


38. Arattupuzha Sastha Temple

[Festival / Ritual]

Arattupuzha Pooram is remembered as one of Kerala's oldest pooram festivals and is often honoured as the mother of all poorams. The festival is centred on Arattupuzha Sastha Temple in Thrissur district, where deities from many neighbouring temples are ceremonially brought together in a divine assembly. Caparisoned elephants, panchavadyam, pandimelam, parasol displays, and the solemn arattu at the river ghat give the festival its grandeur. More than spectacle, the ritual expresses an old belief that the gods and goddesses of the region gather at Arattupuzha in fellowship around Sastha.


39. Ochira Parabrahma Temple

[Festival / Ritual]

Ochira Parabrahma Temple is unique because worship is offered to the formless Parabrahmam without a conventional sanctum or idol. Its best-known ritual festival is Ochira Kali, held in the Malayalam month of Mithunam, where groups of men and boys enact a mock battle in a waterlogged field using sticks instead of weapons. The ritual is connected with memories of old regional warfare and is performed as a vigorous community observance rather than ordinary entertainment. Ochira is also known for Kalakettu or Kalavela, when large decorated bull forms are brought by local communities as devotional offerings connected with the Onam season.


40. Uthralikavu Bhagavathy Temple

[Festival / Ritual]

Uthralikavu Pooram belongs to the Bhagavathy shrine at Wadakkanchery, traditionally associated with Rudhiramahakali worship. The festival is held in the Malayalam month of Kumbham and is known for its day-and-night elephant processions, panchavadyam, pandimelam, colourful parasols, alavattam, venchamaram, and fireworks. The pooram gathers nearby village groups into a shared celebration around the goddess, with each procession carrying its own rhythm and visual identity. Its setting among fields and low hills gives the festival a distinct atmosphere, where devotion, local pride, music, and spectacle come together around Bhagavathy.
